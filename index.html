Inmigration In Finland

Migration Trends & Top Origins

Recent official figures paint a detailed picture of Finland’s immigration (2022–2024). Net migration surged (over +57,000 in 2023) as more people moved in than out. The largest inflows came from Ukraine (especially under temporary protection), Russia, and major labor-sending countries like India, Sri Lanka and Iraq. Migrants arrive on diverse permits: work permits (record highs in 2022, easing in 2023–24), student visas (rising to ~14,000 new applicants in 2024), family reunification, asylum, and temporary protection.

Residence Permit Categories

Immigration occurs under various permit types. Work-based permits: Applications peaked in 2022 (~21,000 first permits) then eased (≈17,000 in 2023, ~15,000 in 2024). The largest work permit holders are from the Philippines, India, Russia and China. Many fill labor shortages (e.g. in healthcare, berry picking, construction). Student permits: Applications climbed sharply (9,855 in 2022 to 12,867 in 2023, 14,163 in 2024), reflecting both law changes and active recruitment of foreign students. Family permits: Tied to others’ status, these also rose. In 2023 there were 21,583 first-time family-permit applications (up from 18,981 in 2022), with about 20,278 permits actually granted (vs 15,457 in 2022). Applicants came mainly from countries already sending workers (Sri Lanka, Russia, India, Philippines, Bangladesh). International protection: Asylum applications have been modest. In 2024 only ~2,400 first-time asylum claims were filed (roughly half of 2023’s level). The peak of 2022–23 was largely Ukrainian Temporary Protection: about 13,000 TP permits were granted in 2024, though applications for TP fell ~35% from 2023 to 2024.

Employment and Unemployment Rates

Official labour surveys highlight stark gaps. In 2024, the unemployment rate among persons with foreign background (ages 20–64) was about 16.7%, versus only ~6.6% for Finnish-background people. Working-age migrants faced roughly 2–3 times higher unemployment. Correspondingly, foreign-background persons make up ~25% of all unemployed 20–64 year-olds (though they are ~12% of that age group). The employment rate (share of 20–64 year-olds working) also differs. Among foreign-background people it rose from the mid-60% range (in 2016–2021) to about 67% in 2022, then fell in 2023–24 with the economic slowdown. Finnish-background employment was higher and steadier.

Reliance on Social Benefits

Migrants also show higher usage of public assistance. Kela (the Finnish Social Insurance Institution) and Statistics Finland data indicate persons of foreign background receive unemployment and housing support at rates well above their population share. For example, in 2022 roughly 9% of Finland’s population had foreign mother tongue, but they made up 26% of unemployment benefit recipients. Similarly, an analysis found 34% of immigrants received housing allowance versus 17% of others. Unemployment benefits went to 26% of immigrants vs 7% of natives, and income support to 22% vs 6%.

Crime and Safety Statistics

Nationwide crime data also record differences by background. According to Statistics Finland, the number of persons of foreign background suspected of criminal offenses grew 17.2% in 2023, versus +19.5% for Finnish-background suspects. In 2017–18, foreign citizens had a suspect rate of about 41.8 per 1,000 residents versus 35.7 per 1,000 Finns. These figures refer to suspects recorded by police, not final convictions. Multiple factors (urban concentration, age/gender structure, reporting practices) influence the statistics.

Social and Fiscal Impacts

The fiscal impact of immigration varies by group. Highly skilled migrants (many from India, the Philippines, China) tend to contribute positively via taxes and filling labor shortages. In contrast, refugees and recent arrivals often need more initial social support. Kela’s research shows that on average foreign-born residents draw about twice the welfare payments of Finnish-born. In benefit terms (unemployment, housing, income support) immigrants used roughly €3,300/year compared to €900 for natives. Many newcomers are young and of working age, mitigating long-term pension costs. Multiple studies suggest that, once integrated, immigrants’ net fiscal contribution can become positive, especially if unemployment gaps close.
